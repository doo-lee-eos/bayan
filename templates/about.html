ABOUT THIS PROJECT
What Bayan Is
Bayan ("بَيَان", clear explanation) is a small tool for pulling apart any word in the Qur'an — prefix, stem, suffix, root — and seeing what each piece actually means, in context, rather than just a single flattened translation.
WHY IT EXISTS

Most translations give you one English sentence per verse. That's enough to follow along, but it hides a lot: which word carries which idea, what root a word shares with other words elsewhere in the text, and how a prefix or suffix quietly changes a meaning.

The goal isn't to hand you a bigger vocabulary list to memorise — it's to build a feel for how the Arabic of the Qur'an actually works. Once you can see that بَرْق (lightning), أَبَارِيق (jugs), and بَرِقَ (dazzled, glistened) all come from the same root, gleaming or flashing, you start recognising that root instinctively wherever it shows up, rather than looking each form up as an unrelated word every time. Bayan surfaces that structure directly, word by word, so the roots and patterns sink in on their own.

HOW SEARCH WORKS

Type a word in any form — fully vocalized, stripped of diacritics, or just its root — and Bayan tries to match it against the exact surface first, then a diacritic-free spelling, then its dictionary (lemma) form, and finally its three-letter root, so a rough or partial spelling still finds the right entry.

  • Word breakdown — every prefix, stem, and suffix attached to a word, glossed individually.
  • Root families — every distinct form a root takes across the Qur'an, grouped together rather than scattered verse by verse.
  • Classical definitions — curated short glosses backed by the full entry from Lane's Arabic-English Lexicon, kept available in full rather than summarized away.
  • Every occurrence — every ayah a word or root appears in, with its own translation alongside.
A NOTE ON ACCURACY

Where the Qur'anic corpus's own annotations are available, Bayan uses those directly. Where they're not, it falls back to automated morphological analysis, which is occasionally wrong. Curated corrections are added over time as they're found — if something looks off, it probably is worth a second look rather than taken as final.
